Community Eco-Credit

Greenfi.org shares open-source knowledge and tools which support use of the "community eco-credit methodology" by informal financial self-help groups.

The Greenfi project developed the community eco-credit green financial instrument and setup methodology, and piloted it with NGO partners in agricultural and coastal settings.

Our approach gives informal financial self-help groups, such as savings groups and chamas, a self-owned and self-managed environmental and financial tool, intended to integrate and build local environmental and financial wealth.

What is community eco-credit?

"Eco-credit" is a loan which requires borrowers to undertake activities which protect or restore nature alongside loan repayment. Nature protection activities can include, for example, soil conservation measures, tree and mangrove planting or fisheries protection. Loans may serve any purpose and are not limited to financing nature restoration or protection activities. Ideally, the required nature protection or restration activities address environmental damage created by the financed activity.

"Community eco-credit" places a revolving credit facility, capitalised via a grant and called an "eco-credit fund", in the hands of informal financial self-help groups. The eco-credit fund, a valuable local financial asset, is self-owned and self-managed. Through the issuance of eco-credit loans, the fund meets the ongoing small credit needs of its members as well as creates a local incentive for nature protection and restoration.

Why community eco-credit is important

People excluded from formal financial channels manage much of the world’s land and seascapes, yet remain least supported with green financial instruments which could derisk and encourage a transition to sustainable resource management practices. Community eco-credit is a first-of-its-kind green financial instrument, designed specifically for people who rely on informal finance to meet their financial needs, and aiming to create locally-owned tools for nature protection and restoration.

Progress

During its development phase, the eco-credit model won numerous innovation awards, including the UN/Morgan Stanley Prize for Climate Change Finance Innovation, and was endorsed by leading governments through the Global Innovation Lab for Climate Finance. Five organisations have used the Greenfi approach to help nearly 300 new and established groups, with 20–30 members each, to operate small revolving eco-credit funds. The methodology is open-source, and designed to be adapted by its users and within different contexts.
